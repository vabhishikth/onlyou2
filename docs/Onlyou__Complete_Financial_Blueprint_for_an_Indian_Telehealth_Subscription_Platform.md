# Onlyou: Complete Financial Blueprint for an Indian Telehealth Subscription Platform

**Onlyou's subscription pricing is broadly competitive across Hair Loss, ED, and PCOS but requires urgent repricing for GLP-1 weight management following generic semaglutide's Indian launch in March 2026.** The "pay after doctor" model — free AI assessment plus free consultation with payment only post-treatment-plan — aligns with industry norms where competitors like Man Matters, Bold Care, and Veera all offer free consultations as acquisition funnels. The critical financial insight: Onlyou's real profit engine is not the consultation but the **medication margin** (the spread between trade price at ~60–65% of MRP and the bundled subscription price), which can yield **40–65% gross margins** on Hair Loss, ED, and PE verticals when structured correctly. However, the mixed-supply risk under GST — where bundling consultation with medicines could attract **18% GST** instead of the healthcare exemption — demands careful invoicing architecture from day one.

## PART 1: Pricing is competitive, but GLP-1 needs a hard reset

### Vertical-by-vertical competitive assessment

Onlyou's pricing sits in a strategically sound middle zone for most verticals. Hair Loss at **₹999/month** positions below Traya's personalized programs (₹1,800–₹3,200/month) while offering more clinical value than Man Matters' OTC kits (₹599–₹1,349/month), which typically exclude prescription medications like Finasteride. The quarterly discount of ~17% and 6-month discount of ~25% align precisely with Indian D2C health subscription norms. ED and PE at **₹1,299/month** fall within the ₹500–₹1,500 range where most competitors operate on a per-product basis, though PE commands a premium that must be justified through clear clinical differentiation — Onlyou prescribes Dapoxetine (genuine Rx treatment) versus competitors selling OTC lidocaine delay sprays at ₹299–₹499.

PCOS at **₹1,499/month** is well-positioned below Veera Health (₹1,583–₹2,567/month for comparable programs) while offering far more than Bodywise's supplement-only approach at ₹300/month. Weight Standard at **₹2,999/month** is premium — roughly 2× above supplement-only competitors — but justified if it includes prescription medications (Orlistat, Metformin) plus clinical monitoring.

**The GLP-1 vertical at ₹9,999/month faces a structural pricing problem.** Generic semaglutide injectable entered India in March 2026 following Novo Nordisk's patent expiry, with Natco pricing at ₹1,290/month, Alkem at ₹1,800/month, and Dr. Reddy's Obeda pen at ₹4,200/month. Over 40 Indian pharma companies have launched generics. Onlyou's ₹9,999/month made sense in a pre-generic world where branded Ozempic cost ₹8,800–₹11,175/month, but with medication now available from ₹1,290/month, the subscription should be repriced to **₹4,999–₹6,999/month** (generic medication + clinical management + delivery + monitoring).

### Where the real profit comes from

The primary profit driver is the **medication margin** — the difference between what Onlyou pays at trade price (~60–65% of MRP) and what's effectively charged through the subscription. For ED medications, this margin can exceed **₹1,000/month** because generics like Sildenafil cost as little as ₹20–50 for 4 tablets at trade price.

Secondary revenue streams include **lab test margins** (diagnostic labs offer B2B pricing at 40–60% below MRP; a ₹500 retail thyroid panel can be procured for ₹150–250), **cross-vertical subscriptions** (each additional vertical increases LTV by 30–50%), **supplement upsells** (60–70% gross margin on branded supplements), and eventually **corporate wellness programs** (₹200–₹1,500 per employee per month with longer sales cycles but lower churn). Data monetization under DPDPA is not a near-term viable revenue stream due to regulatory complexity.

## PART 2: GST structure demands separate invoicing from day one

### The consultation exemption is real but fragile

Healthcare services provided by an authorized medical practitioner are **exempt from GST** under Notification No. 12/2017-Central Tax (Rate), Entry No. 74. The Gujarat AAAR explicitly confirmed in *Baroda Medicare (GUJ-AAAR-09-2022)* that services provided "by phone, internet or other means" qualify for this exemption. Telehealth consultation by a Registered Medical Practitioner is not OIDAR (Online Information and Database Access or Retrieval services) — it involves human professional intervention and the internet is merely the delivery medium.

However, the **biggest tax risk is the mixed-supply classification**. The AAR Haryana ruling in *Source of Life Technologies (HAR/HAAR/R/2018-19/16)* held that telemedicine bundled with a medical kit constitutes a **mixed supply** under Section 2(74) CGST Act — not a composite supply — because telemedicine and physical products are independently available. If a subscription bundling consultation + medicines + delivery is treated as mixed supply, **GST at 18%** (the highest rate among components) applies to the entire amount. This would devastate unit economics.

**The recommended structure: issue a single invoice with itemized line items.** Consultation fee (SAC 999312) as exempt, prescribed medicines (HSN 3004) at **5% GST**, and delivery charges (SAC 9965) at **18% GST**. This avoids the mixed-supply trap by demonstrating that each component is a separately identifiable supply. The 56th GST Council (September 2025) rationalized pharmaceutical GST, moving most medicines previously at 12% down to **5%**. Most drugs in Onlyou's portfolio — Finasteride, Sildenafil, Tadalafil, Dapoxetine, Orlistat, Semaglutide — fall under HSN 3004 at 5%.

### TDS on doctor payments follows Section 194J at 10%

Payments to doctors as independent consultants attract **TDS at 10%** under Section 194J (professional services). The threshold is **₹50,000 per annum per doctor** (increased from ₹30,000 by Finance Act 2025). Multiple tribunal decisions — *ACIT v. Fortis Healthcare*, *CIT(TDS) v. Apollo Hospitals International* — have consistently classified doctor payments under 194J, not 194C (contractor). CBDT Circular No. 8/2009 clarifies that only professional service fees attract 194J; reimbursements for medicines or equipment are excluded.

Compliance requires quarterly filing of **Form 26Q** (due 31st of the month following quarter-end), monthly TDS deposit by the 7th of the following month, and issuing **Form 16A** certificates to each doctor within 15 days of the Form 26Q due date. For doctors providing healthcare services, their fee is **GST-exempt** per CBIC Circular No. 32/06/2018-GST, so no GST is charged on doctor invoices.

### Input Tax Credit creates a complex proportional reversal scenario

Because Onlyou has both exempt outputs (consultation) and taxable outputs (medicine sales at 5%, delivery at 18%), **proportional ITC reversal under Rules 42 and 43 of CGST Rules** is mandatory. The formula requires segregating total ITC into exclusively taxable, exclusively exempt, and common credit pools, then reversing the exempt proportion of common credits monthly in GSTR-3B. ITC is available on Razorpay gateway fees (18% GST on the 2% fee), delivery fleet expenses, cloud hosting, and other business inputs — but not on doctor fees (exempt healthcare) or employee food (blocked credit under Section 17(5)).

**Payment gateway impact on unit economics**: Razorpay charges 2% on cards/net banking plus 18% GST on the fee, totaling ~2.36% effective rate. For subscription recurring payments, an additional ~0.99% subscription management fee applies, bringing total gateway cost to **~3.5% of transaction value** for card payments. UPI AutoPay carries zero MDR under NPCI mandate (though Razorpay may charge a platform fee). **Recommendation: push UPI AutoPay as the primary payment method.** For a ₹999/month subscription on card, gateway costs approximately ₹35; on UPI, potentially ₹0–10.

### Drug pricing regulations favor Onlyou's portfolio

Most drugs in Onlyou's formulary — Finasteride, Minoxidil (topical), Sildenafil, Tadalafil, Dapoxetine, Orlistat, Semaglutide — are **not on the NLEM 2022** and are not under DPCO price control. The exceptions are **Metformin** (essential medicine, ceiling price controlled), **basic OCPs** (Ethinylestradiol + Levonorgestrel combinations), and **Spironolactone** (listed as cardiovascular medicine). For DPCO-scheduled drugs, the ceiling price includes a mandated **16% retailer margin** and adjusts annually via WPI — the April 2026 adjustment was **+0.65%**. Non-scheduled drugs face a maximum **10% annual MRP increase** under DPCO 2013.

No one can charge above MRP under the Legal Metrology Act 2009. However, the subscription model provides pricing flexibility because it is structured as a **bundled healthcare service** (consultation + medication management + delivery), not as a pure medicine sale. A separate consultation fee or platform fee is legally permissible provided it is transparently disclosed. All competitors — PharmEasy, 1mg, Man Matters — charge delivery or convenience fees in various forms.

## PART 3: Partner payments require three distinct settlement models

### Pharmacy partner — the revenue-share-plus-dispensing-fee model

Since Onlyou operates without a Drug License, the partner pharmacy must dispense all medications. Three models exist in Indian e-pharmacy:

**Model 1 (Recommended): Fixed dispensing fee + margin share.** The pharmacy dispenses medications, receives a fixed ₹30–50 per order dispensing fee, and the platform collects the full subscription from the patient. The pharmacy invoices Onlyou at **trade price** (PTR, approximately 60–65% of MRP) plus 5% GST on medicines. Onlyou retains the spread between what the patient pays (embedded in subscription) and trade price + dispensing fee. Settlement should occur **weekly** to maintain pharmacy relationship health. For returns and damaged goods, the pharmacy handles per state drug rules; the platform credits the customer and debits the pharmacy's settlement account. GST invoicing flows as: pharmacy issues tax invoice to Onlyou for medicines (HSN 3004, 5% GST), and Onlyou claims ITC on these purchases.

**Model 2 (Alternative): Commission model.** The pharmacy dispenses at MRP, collects payment from the platform, and pays Onlyou an 8–15% commission. This is how PharmEasy operates with its 18,000+ partner pharmacies. Less suitable for Onlyou's subscription model because the platform needs to control pricing.

### Doctor payment — hybrid async consultation model

For Onlyou's async (text + photo) model, the optimal doctor compensation structure is a **per-consultation fee with a monthly minimum guarantee**:

- **Initial consultation (async):** ₹200–₹350 per patient — below in-person specialist rates (₹500–₹2,000) but reflecting the async efficiency advantage where doctors can review cases at convenience
- **Follow-up check-ins (4-week, 3-month, 6-month):** ₹100–₹200 per check-in — shorter review time
- **Unlimited messaging responses:** Bundled into a monthly retainer of ₹15,000–₹25,000 for a part-time specialist handling 150–300 patients
- **Monthly minimum guarantee:** ₹20,000–₹30,000 for part-time specialists committed to specific hours

The free consultation model means Onlyou absorbs doctor costs for patients who don't convert. With industry conversion rates of **15–30%** from free consultation to paid subscription, the effective doctor cost per acquired subscriber is ₹667–₹2,333 (₹200–₹350 consultation fee ÷ 15–30% conversion rate). This should be booked as a **customer acquisition expense**, not COGS. MFine's model provides a benchmark: doctors charged ₹600/consultation with the platform retaining ₹50 and the doctor receiving ₹550. Practo takes ~37% commission. For async, rates should be 30–50% lower than synchronous video consultations.

TDS at 10% under Section 194J applies to all doctor payments exceeding ₹50,000 annually. Doctor services are GST-exempt as healthcare services by an authorized medical practitioner, so no GST is charged on their invoices.

### Nurse, lab, and delivery fleet settlement structures

**Phlebotomists** for blood draws should be paid ₹150–₹250 per home visit (salary allocation ₹50–100 + travel allowance ₹50–100 + consumables ₹30–50). Average phlebotomist salary in India is ₹14,000–₹25,000/month. Failed visits (patient unavailable) should be compensated at 50% of standard rate plus full travel allowance. TDS under Section 194C at 1% (individual) or 2% (HUF/company) applies if using contract phlebotomists.

**Diagnostic lab partners** offer B2B pricing at **40–60% below MRP**. A CBC test retailing at ₹350–₹600 can be procured for ₹100–₹200 at B2B rates. Settlement cycles with labs like Thyrocare, SRL, or Metropolis are typically **15–30 days**. Re-collection costs are absorbed by the lab if the sample was inadequate due to lab error; by the platform if the patient was non-compliant with pre-test instructions. Thyrocare's franchise model offers "best margin in the industry" with workload-linked discounts and slab-based incentives.

**Delivery fleet (own fleet)** should operate on a **salary + per-delivery incentive model**: base salary of ₹18,000–₹25,000/month for 15–20 deliveries/day, plus ₹15–₹25 per-delivery incentive above the daily target. Cold chain delivery premium for GLP-1 injectables: additional ₹25–₹50 per delivery for handling insulated packaging. Total cost per delivery works out to ₹85–₹90 for standard orders and ₹150–₹200 for cold chain. Fuel reimbursement at actuals or ₹2,500–₹3,500/month fixed allowance.

## PART 4: Ten edge cases that will break your finance model if unplanned

### 1. The ₹999 Hair Loss waterfall — where every rupee goes

For a single ₹999/month Hair Loss subscriber paying via UPI AutoPay:

| Recipient | Amount | % of Revenue |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| GST liability (blended ~4.5%) | ₹45 | 4.5% |
| Payment gateway (UPI ~0.5%) | ₹5 | 0.5% |
| Partner pharmacy (trade price + dispensing) | ₹420–₹600 | 42–60% |
| Doctor (amortized across consults) | ₹120–₹150 | 12–15% |
| Delivery (own fleet) | ₹85–₹90 | 8.5–9% |
| Platform tech costs | ₹20–₹37 | 2–3.7% |
| **Onlyou retained margin** | **₹67–₹304** | **6.7–30.4%** |

The margin is thin on Hair Loss at monthly pricing. **Quarterly and 6-month subscriptions are where profitability materializes**: at ₹4,499/6-month (₹750/month effective), Onlyou locks in the customer while reducing effective CAC per month, though per-month margin compresses further. The strategy must be to convert monthly subscribers to longer plans.

### 2. Multi-vertical subscriptions — bundle with a single composite invoice

When a patient subscribes to both Hair Loss (₹999) and ED (₹1,299), the recommended approach is: **single invoice, itemized lines, with a 10–15% bundle discount** (₹1,953–₹2,068 combined versus ₹2,298 total). Issue one composite invoice with consultation component (exempt), Hair Loss medicines (5% GST), ED medicines (5% GST), and delivery (one delivery, 18% GST). Bundle discount reduces customer-facing price while each medication component maintains its own GST classification.

### 3. Failed subscription renewal — platform absorbs the gap

When payment fails and succeeds after retry (up to 3 retries over 7–10 days per RBI norms), **Onlyou absorbs the delay**: the patient's medication shipment is held, and the subscription period extends from the successful payment date, not the original renewal date. No cost is passed to pharmacy or doctor partners. Under RBI e-mandate rules, pre-debit notification must be sent 24 hours before every recurring charge. For subscriptions above ₹15,000 (only GLP-1 quarterly at ₹24,999 and 6-month plans), additional OTP authentication is required each cycle, increasing payment failure rates.

### 4. Mid-cycle cancellation requires GST credit note within strict timelines

Prorated refund = (Unused days ÷ Total subscription days) × Subscription price. A credit note under **Section 34 CGST Act** must be issued before the GSTR-1 filing deadline of September of the following financial year. The credit note reverses GST liability in GSTR-3B. **Recommendation**: default to wallet credits (6-month validity) instead of direct refunds — wallet credits are treated as advance payments, avoiding immediate GST reversal complexity. Offer direct bank refund only on explicit request for amounts above ₹1,000.

### 5. Lab tests during free consultation — book as customer acquisition cost

When a doctor orders a blood panel during the free (pre-conversion) consultation, this cost is borne entirely by Onlyou. Book under a dedicated cost center **"CAC — Free Consultation Diagnostics"** as a marketing expense, not COGS. At B2B lab pricing (40–60% below MRP), a basic thyroid panel costs ₹150–₹250, a CBC costs ₹100–₹150. If the "first blood panel free" offer extends post-conversion, track it as a subscriber benefit cost under COGS. Claim ITC on the GST component of lab invoices.

### 6. GLP-1 negative margin risk — build a medication cost cap

If generic semaglutide costs ₹4,200–₹4,500/month (premium pen devices like Dr. Reddy's Obeda) while the subscription is priced at ₹5,999, the margin after doctor, delivery, and cold chain costs could turn negative. **Solution**: establish a medication cost cap in the subscription terms (e.g., "covers medications up to ₹X/month") and require a co-pay for premium branded or pen-device medications. Alternatively, default to the cheapest clinically appropriate generic (Natco vial at ₹1,290/month) and offer premium pen devices as an upgrade tier at ₹8,999–₹9,999/month.

### 7. NPPA ceiling price revision mid-subscription

For DPCO-controlled drugs in Onlyou's portfolio (Metformin, basic OCPs, Spironolactone), ceiling prices adjust annually via WPI. The April 2026 adjustment was +0.65%. **Contract structure**: pharmacy partner agreements should specify MRP-based pricing with automatic adjustment per NPPA notifications. For non-scheduled drugs (maximum 10% annual MRP increase), build quarterly price review clauses into subscription terms with 30-day advance notice to subscribers before any increase.

### 8–10. Pharmacy termination, wallet GST, and multi-city expansion

Pharmacy partner termination requires **30–60 days notice**, settlement of all pending invoices within 15 business days, and a patient transition plan. Existing medication stock belongs to the pharmacy. Wallet credits from promotional campaigns carry **no GST liability** at issuance (no consideration); GST applies only at redemption against a supply. For cross-city expansion, maintain city-wise P&L tracking with a national subscription price but negotiate city-specific vendor rates, tracking **CM2 (Contribution Margin after fulfillment) by city** to identify unprofitable geographies before scaling.

## PART 5: The recommended financial architecture for launch

### Optimal doctor compensation — async hybrid model

Deploy **3–5 part-time specialists per vertical** (dermatologists for Hair Loss, urologists for ED/PE, endocrinologists for Weight, gynecologists for PCOS) on a hybrid model: monthly retainer of ₹20,000–₹30,000 guaranteeing 10–15 hours/week availability, plus ₹150–₹250 per initial async consultation and ₹75–₹150 per follow-up. At 1,000 subscribers across verticals, this means approximately 8–12 doctors with a total monthly doctor payroll of ₹1.6–₹3.6 lakh. Each async doctor can handle **40–60 consultations per day** (3–5 minutes per case review for text + photo), making this highly scalable. For free consultations that don't convert, amortize the doctor cost across converted subscribers as CAC.

### Optimal pharmacy partner — single partner with expansion clause

Start with a **single pharmacy partner** in the MVP city, negotiated on a **trade price + ₹40 dispensing fee per order** model. This gives Onlyou maximum margin control. The pharmacy invoices at PTR (~60–65% of MRP) plus 5% GST, with weekly settlement. Contract should include: exclusive dispensing rights for the platform's prescriptions in the city, minimum order volume commitments (500+ orders/month for better trade pricing), quality SLA (dispensing accuracy >99.5%, turnaround <4 hours), and a 60-day termination clause with transition support. At scale (5,000+ subscribers), introduce a second pharmacy partner for redundancy and negotiate volume-based trade discounts to 55–60% of MRP.

### GST registration and compliance architecture

Register as a **Regular Taxpayer** (not Composition Scheme, which is unavailable for service providers above ₹50 lakh or for interstate supplies). Obtain GSTIN in the state of principal place of business. File **GSTR-1 monthly** (outward supplies, due 11th), **GSTR-3B monthly** (summary with tax payment, due 20th), and **GSTR-9 annually** (due December 31st). E-invoicing is not mandatory until aggregate turnover exceeds ₹5 crore, but voluntary adoption using Zoho Books' IRP integration is recommended for investor-readiness.

Use SAC **999312** for consultation services (exempt), HSN **3004** for medicines (5% GST), and SAC **996812** for delivery (18% GST). For TDS, file **Form 26Q quarterly** for doctor payments under Section 194J, deposit TDS by the 7th of each following month, and issue Form 16A certificates within 15 days of 26Q due date.

### Seven financial KPIs to track from day one

Track **MRR and vertical-wise revenue split** (target: ₹2–5 crore ARR for Series A conversations). Monitor **CAC and LTV:CAC ratio** (target: ≥3:1; with ₹800–₹1,500 paid CAC and 8-month average subscriber tenure at ₹1,500 ARPU, LTV of ~₹12,000 yields a healthy 8–15× ratio). Watch **monthly churn rate** closely (target: <5%; above 5% "is a serious problem that should take priority over growth" per investor benchmarks). Calculate **CM1** (revenue minus COGS) and **CM2** (CM1 minus fulfillment costs) per vertical per city — these are the unit economics that investors scrutinize. Track **free-to-paid conversion rate** (target: 20–30%) as the key operational efficiency metric for the "pay after doctor" model. Finally, maintain a **GST/TDS compliance scorecard** — 100% on-time filing is non-negotiable for fundraising due diligence.

## Conclusion: Onlyou's model works if the invoicing architecture is right

The "pay after doctor" model is operationally sound and aligns with industry practice — every major Indian telehealth competitor offers free consultations as acquisition funnels. The critical financial insight is that **the invoicing structure determines whether the business pays 0% or 18% GST on its core revenue**. Separate line-item invoicing (consultation exempt + medicines at 5% + delivery at 18%) versus bundled single-rate billing is a decision worth lakhs per month at scale. The GLP-1 repricing is urgent — generic semaglutide has fundamentally altered the unit economics, and the current ₹9,999/month positions Onlyou as overpriced against a rapidly commoditizing market. PE and ED are the margin engines; Hair Loss and PCOS are the volume drivers. Build the financial model around this asymmetry, cross-sell aggressively across verticals, and target positive CM2 across all verticals within 6 months of launch.
